Historical Through-Line

500+ Years.
One Lie.
Still Here.

9th Century B.C. · Biblical Origins

The Name: Jezebel, Queen of Israel

A Phoenician princess depicted in the books of Kings as deceitful and immoral. Her name becomes synonymous with feminine wickedness — the original template that later colonists would reach for.

Late 1500s · Elizabethan Era

The "Lusty Moor" — A Colonial Tool

English colonists adopt the image of the "lusty Moor" to argue Black people are subhuman. The characterization provides ideological cover for enslavement before it even begins in the Americas.

Early 1630s · Slavery Era

The Stereotype Takes Root in the Colonies

European travelers misrepresent African dress and social customs as evidence of uncontrolled sexual lust — giving the myth a veneer of "observation."

1600s–1800s · Slavery Era

The Jezebel Myth Is Built to Protect Rapists

White enslavers construct the Jezebel narrative to justify the systematic rape of enslaved Black women. If she is "always willing," there is no crime. The 18th century adds a pseudo-scientific layer. The law agrees. This is the blueprint.

1800s · Antebellum South

The Placage System & Forced Reproduction

Wealthy white Southerners formalize exploitation through the Placage system. Enslaved Black women are forced to be "breeders" for future labor supply; their resulting high birth rates are then weaponized as "proof" of sexual promiscuity.

1800s · Victorian Era

The Two-Tiered Standard Is Engineered

White women are elevated onto pedestals of purity and innocence. Black women are deliberately excluded. While white femininity is legally and socially protected, Black women's bodies remain public property — the contrast is not incidental, it is designed.

1810–1815 · London & Paris

Sarah Baartman Displayed for White Audiences

The Khoi woman known as Sarah Baartman is exhibited in European freak shows. White audiences pay to gawk, to touch. Her body becomes spectacle — the white gaze made literal.

1865–Present · Post-Emancipation

The System Evolves, The Lie Persists

Mass incarceration, redlining, economic exclusion, education disparities — new mechanisms sever Black fathers from families. Black women remain unprotected in courts, in churches, online and in communities. The Jezebel stereotype adapts to each era but never disappears.

Today · 2026

The Jezebel in the Current Day

She is in dating apps, music videos, classrooms, clothing, and in the questions asked before "are you okay?" She lives in the way Black women police themselves — in the bedroom, the boardroom, and their own minds. The stereotype is over 500 years old. It is still doing its job.

About This Study

What This Is, And Why It Matters

This project is an independent study created by Shaiyan Sanchez. Originally I was trying to examine the tensions between Black women reaching sexual liberation and enjoying their own sexual pleasure. One of the sidetracks taken in reaching that overarching question was the history. I sat with my faculty advisor and great mentor and shared my ideas. She asked me if I knew what the Jezebel stereotype was and I had no clue. From there this project rose.

I learned so much that contextualized a lot of my own realities and experiences. I wanted to create a project that spoke to young Black women in my communities who haven't heard of this. As Black women navigating a world of white supremacy, capitalism, racism and all the isms — it is important to not let that hold power over us. To allow ourselves to live, to be uniquely us.

Let's educate ourselves, our community, and pour into each other. That's where our power lies.

Themes Across the Study

  • Historical ConstructionHow the Jezebel myth was deliberately built to justify exploitation — and why that history is still largely unknown inside the Black community itself.
  • The White GazeHow whiteness as a standard — not just as people — became internalized, showing up in Black women's most intimate moments.
  • Religion & RespectabilityHow the church inherited the stereotype and encoded it in the language used to police Black girls' bodies — and how elite education did the same.
  • Fatherhood & ProtectionHow the deliberate dismantling of Black fatherhood left Black daughters without the first mirror of their own worth.
  • Violation & DisbeliefHow the Jezebel stereotype was the original legal justification for denying Black women the right to say no — and how that doubt lives on.
  • Intergenerational HealingThe radical act of Black women choosing to give what they were never given — and what that means for the next generation.

Essential Reading

Book · Spirituality & Healing

Sacred Woman: A Guide to Healing the Feminine Mind, Body and Soul

Queen Afua

A transformative guide rooted in African spiritual tradition that speaks directly to Black women's healing — body, mind, and ancestral memory. A cornerstone text for understanding liberation as a sacred, embodied practice.

Book · Media Studies & Race

Reading Pornography and Race

Jennifer Nash

Nash's analysis examines how Black women's bodies are constructed and consumed in pornography — and what that means for identity, pleasure, and the white gaze. A rigorous and necessary read for anyone studying Black women's sexuality in media.

Essay · Feminist Theory

Uses of the Erotic: The Erotic as Power

Audre Lorde, 1978

Lorde's landmark essay argues that the erotic — pleasure, desire, deep feeling — is not a source of shame but a source of power and self-knowledge for women. A counterweight to everything this study excavates, and one of the most important texts in Black feminist thought.

Book · Anti-Racism

White Fragility: Why It's So Hard for White People to Talk About Racism

Robin DiAngelo, 2018

DiAngelo's examination of how white discomfort functions to shut down conversations about race — essential context for understanding why the truths at the center of this study are so rarely spoken aloud in mixed spaces.

Book · Black Feminism & Politics

How We Get Free: Black Feminism and the Combahee River Collective

Keeanga-Yamahtta Taylor (Editor)

A powerful collection tracing the legacy of the Combahee River Collective — the radical Black feminist organization that insisted liberation must be intersectional. Essential reading for understanding the roots of Black feminist thought and political organizing.

Book · Black Panther Party History

Comrade Sisters: Women of the Black Panther Party

Ericka Huggins & Stephen Shames

A powerful tribute to the women who built, sustained, and defined the Black Panther Party — told through Stephen Shames' photographs and Ericka Huggins' words. A recovery of the women whose labor and courage shaped one of the most significant liberation movements in American history.

Book · Race, Gender & American History

When and Where I Enter: The Impact of Black Women on Race and Sex in America

Paula J. Giddings

A landmark work of Black women's history — tracing the ways Black women have shaped American politics, civil rights, and feminist movements from the 19th century to the present. Essential context for understanding Black women as architects of their own liberation.

Book · Black Feminist History

Wayward Lives, Beautiful Experiments: Intimate Histories of Riotous Black Girls, Troublesome Women, and Queer Radicals

Saidiya Hartman

Hartman's groundbreaking work recovers the intimate lives of Black women and girls in early 20th century American cities — their refusals, their pleasures, their attempts to remake the world on their own terms. A stunning act of radical history-telling.

Autobiography · Black Liberation

Assata: An Autobiography

Assata Shakur

The autobiography of revolutionary Black liberation activist Assata Shakur — a first-person account of resistance, state violence, survival, and what it means for a Black woman to claim her own story in a system designed to silence her.
